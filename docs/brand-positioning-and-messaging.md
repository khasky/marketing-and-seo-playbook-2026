# Brand, positioning, and messaging

Who you serve, what you promise, and how you say it — upstream of channels, SEO, and ads. Tie [research](research-and-strategy.md) to [landing copy](landing-pages.md) and [audience pages](marketing-pages-and-attribution.md).

## Ideal customer profile (ICP)

Define firmographic and behavioral filters:

- Segment — company size, geography, industry, tech stack, regulatory context.
- Buyer vs user — economic buyer, champion, daily user; different pains and proof.
- Triggers — what makes them look for a solution now (migration, headcount, compliance, funding).

"Everyone" positioning rarely works; a narrower ICP tightens copy, roadmap, and CAC.

## Positioning statement (working format)

A common pattern:

> For **[target customers]** who **[need or struggle]**, **[product]** is a **[category]** that **[key benefit]**. Unlike **[alternatives]**, we **[differentiator].**

Iterate with sales and support; test in landing headlines and decks.

## Value proposition vs tagline

| Layer             | Role                                                                              |
| ----------------- | --------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| Value proposition | Clear outcome for the ICP (often one sentence plus proof).                        |
| Category          | The mental shelf you occupy (only invent a category if you can teach the market). |
| Tagline           | Memorable; helps recall — not a substitute for clarity on the pricing page.       |

Keep claims defensible; match big promises to [Legal — compliance](legal-compliance.md).

## Messaging hierarchy

1. Company — mission, trust, who you are.
2. Product — primary outcome and "for whom."
3. Feature — how a capability delivers the outcome ([feature pages](landing-pages.md)).
4. Campaign — time-bound offer or narrative ([UTMs](marketing-pages-and-attribution.md)).

One primary idea per page; secondary points support, not compete.

## Differentiation axes

Pick two or three dimensions you can own:

- Workflow — speed, depth of integration, collaboration.
- Economics — transparent pricing, usage-based vs seat.
- Risk and trust — security, compliance, support SLAs.
- Audience — built for agencies vs in-house vs enterprise.

Map competitors on a simple 2×2; avoid claiming you win on every axis.

## Proof and evidence

- Customer logos, quotes, case studies ([Landing — case studies](landing-pages.md)).
- Third party — reviews, analyst mentions, badges.
- Product-led — interactive demo, trial, sandbox.

E-E-A-T for SEO needs real substance, not adjectives alone — see [Content — author blocks](content-seo-and-blog.md).

## Naming and voice

- Product name — memorable, pronounceable, searchable; check trademark and domain early.
- Voice — document tone (formal vs friendly), words to avoid, inclusive language.
- Localization — if you sell in several languages, plan transcreation, not literal translation ([international signals](technical-seo-and-site-health.md)).

## Sales and marketing alignment

Share a one-pager: ICP, positioning, top objections, win/loss reasons. Refresh after quarterly reviews so site copy, ads, and outbound stay aligned.
