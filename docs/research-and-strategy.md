# Research and startup strategy

Validation, failure modes, funding, entity setup, and signals of a strong product or launch. When you move from problem clarity to market story, use [Brand, positioning and messaging](brand-positioning-and-messaging.md).

## Why startups fail

Common failure themes show up across several widely cited frameworks:

**Eight recurring reasons**

1. Lack of product–market fit (PMF)
2. Running out of cash
3. No clear business model
4. Neglecting marketing and sales
5. Failing to hire the right people
6. Not adapting to change
7. Mismanagement of growth
8. Lack of focus

**Additional patterns (market and execution)**

- Misunderstanding the market; timing and external shocks matter.
- Cash, flawed plans, bad partnerships, and loss of founder passion show up often in post-mortems.
- Hiring too fast or before repeatable sales can burn runway.

**Practical anti-patterns**

- Delaying launch to ship "just one more feature" instead of learning from real users.
- Targeting too many use cases at once instead of a wedge segment you can win.

Further reading: [Investopedia — how many startups fail](https://www.investopedia.com/articles/personal-finance/040915/how-many-startups-fail-and-why.asp), [HBR — why start-ups fail](https://hbr.org/2021/05/why-start-ups-fail), [Ring Central — 10 reasons](https://www.ringcentral.com/us/en/blog/why-startups-fail/).

## Niche research and profitable niches

### What "profitable niche" means

A workable niche usually has:

- Reachable audience — you can find them in communities, search, or paid channels without impossible CAC.
- Willing buyers — people already pay for substitutes, tools, or services.
- Enough depth — problem recurs; not a one-off curiosity.

### Validation loop

1. Name the problem narrowly — e.g. "scheduling UGC for e-commerce brands on TikTok," not "marketing tools."
2. Listen where they talk — Reddit, LinkedIn, Slack communities, niche Facebook groups, support forums, G2/Capterra reviews of incumbents.
3. Check demand signals — search intent (see [SEO guide](seo-tools-and-faq.md)), trends (e.g. Google Trends), competitor pricing and longevity.
4. Prove spending — competitors, agencies, or ad spend in the category imply budget exists.
5. Test cheaply — landing plus waitlist, concierge MVP, paid micro-campaigns (CTR and opt-in rate tell you if the hook works).

### Tools (starter set)

Keyword / SERP tools (Ahrefs, Semrush, etc.), Google Trends, AnswerThePublic, alerts for brand and problem keywords. Tie keyword work to the intent section in the SEO chapter.

### Niche scorecard (use before you commit build)

Rate each item 1–5 (honest) and fix the worst gaps first:

| Question                                                                     | Why it matters                                     |
| ---------------------------------------------------------------------------- | -------------------------------------------------- |
| Can you name 20 real people (or companies) who already have this problem?    | If not, the niche may be imaginary or unreachable. |
| Do people pay today for a partial solution (tool, agency, salary time)?      | No budget = hobby market.                          |
| Is Google / community search volume or paid CPC visible for core queries?    | Confirms language-market fit.                      |
| Can you reach them under your target CAC in a test (content, outbound, ads)? | Proves channels exist.                             |
| Regulatory or trust barriers (health, finance, children)?                    | Adds cost; plan compliance early.                  |

### Red flags

- Everyone is a customer; no ICP.
- Only competitor is a spreadsheet and no one upgrades.
- You need education from zero on why the problem exists (sometimes OK, often slow).

## What investors look for (early stage)

Investors usually weigh team, market, product, and traction together. For early stage:

- Retention and engagement often weigh more than raw signups: repeat use, activation milestones, cohort behavior.
- Organic or efficient acquisition beats one-off spikes bought with unsustainable spend.
- PMF proxies — e.g. share of users who would be "very disappointed" without the product (Sean Ellis–style survey), depth of usage, expansion within accounts.
- Unit economics story — even if revenue is small, show you understand LTV/CAC, payback, and churn.
- Clarity — what wedge you own first, why now, why you.

Vanity metrics (followers alone, empty beta list, one viral post) rarely substitute for repeated usage or revenue.

### Typical diligence questions (prepare one slide each)

| Theme   | What to show                                                   |
| ------- | -------------------------------------------------------------- |
| Why you | Founders' edge (distribution, domain, technical).              |
| Market  | Bottom-up TAM / SAM (how you got the number).                  |
| Proof   | Cohort retention, activation rate, paid pilots, LOIs.          |
| Risk    | What you need to learn next 90 days and how you'll measure it. |

Useful deep dives: [Allied VC — measuring traction](https://www.allied.vc/guides/measuring-early-stage-startup-traction), [CRV — what seed investors look for](https://www.crv.com/content/what-seed-investors-look-for) (examples; firms differ).

## How to open a US company as a non-resident

*Operational overview only, not tax or legal advice.*

Many non-US founders use:

- Delaware LLC or C-Corp — common for SaaS; Delaware has well-developed corporate law.
- Registered agent — required in the formation state.
- EIN — needed for banking and many US platforms.
- Services — e.g. Stripe Atlas (incorporation, documents, banking/payments onboarding stack), plus traditional law firms and filing agents.

**Stripe Atlas** (documentation: [Stripe Atlas docs](https://docs.stripe.com/atlas)) bundles incorporation, EIN, equity templates, and partner perks — compare with counsel for your situation.

### Typical steps (high level — verify with counsel)

1. Choose entity — LLC vs C-Corp (fundraising and tax tradeoffs).
2. File with state (e.g. Delaware) plus registered agent.
3. Obtain EIN from the IRS (often via fax or SS-4 for foreign persons — process varies).
4. Open US bank (often hardest step; Atlas and some banks specialize in non-resident founders).
5. Connect payments — Stripe, etc., with KYC aligned to the entity.
6. Ongoing compliance — annual report, franchise tax, registered agent renewal.

### Non-US founder tax traps (non-exhaustive)

- US trade or business and effectively connected income.
- Withholding on US-source payments.
- Treaty positions and tax residency in your home country.

Always confirm tax residency, treaty, and substance rules with a CPA and lawyer who work with cross-border founders.

Further reading: [Stripe — LLC for non-residents](https://stripe.com/resources/more/how-to-open-an-llc-in-the-usa-for-nonresidents), [Delaware LLC overview](https://stripe.com/en-at/resources/more/delaware-llcs-a-guide-to-getting-started)

## Successful product / launch heuristics

Illustrative pattern: FaceApp-style breakout often combines novelty, fast iteration, and broad demographic appeal. These are heuristics, not guarantees:

- Niche or differentiated product; or clear angle vs incumbents.
- Fast implementation (roughly under ~12 months to a meaningful version).
- Simple, performant UX.
- Avoid heavy moderation or extreme security burden if you are resource-constrained.
- Wide age appeal can expand TAM (with appropriate compliance for minors where relevant).
- Trials should show a sharp productivity or delight delta.
- Strong onboarding / walkthrough (see [Product UX, analytics, and support](product-and-ux.md)).
- If competitors exist, mine their reviews for switchers and churn reasons.

**Idea theft:** mitigate with execution speed, trade secrets, and legal instruments where appropriate — see [Legal](legal-compliance.md) for NDAs; patents are sector-specific.

## App onboarding / walkthrough

Study step-by-step onboarding (e.g. Hootsuite-style guided flows). Review how scheduling and social tools structure first-run experiences — many products (PlannThat, Sprout Social, SocialPilot, Kontentino, Tailwind, and similar) use checklists, tooltips, and empty states to drive time-to-value. Principles:

- Time-to-value in minutes, not days.
- Progressive disclosure — do not teach every feature on day one.
- Checklists with a few high-impact tasks; celebrate completion.
